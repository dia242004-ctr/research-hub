01

How they make money

The classic NBFC spread engine — borrow wholesale, lend granular, earn the gap.

Tata Capital is a non-bank lender: it has no retail deposits, so it borrows wholesale (bonds, bank loans, CPs) at a cost of funds of ~7.1% and lends across retail, SME and corporate segments at higher yields. The gap is Net Interest Income (NII) / net interest margin — the core profit engine — topped up with fees (processing, distribution, insurance/wealth cross-sell).

  • Scale & brand: India's 3rd-largest diversified NBFC — ~900+ branches, ~7 million customers, controlled by Tata Sons. The Tata name is a genuine moat: it lowers the cost of funds and builds borrower trust.
  • Diversification is the strategy: 25+ products across segments, with no single product >20% of the book — deliberately spreading risk across cycles, geographies and sectors.
  • The trade-off: that diversified, lower-yield, high-quality mix is why returns (RoA/RoE) trail higher-yield peers like Bajaj Finance — see §7.
02

Why it listed — RBI's scale-based regulation

This was a regulation-driven IPO, not a capital-need one.

In October 2021 the RBI introduced Scale-Based Regulation (SBR), sorting NBFCs into four tiers by size/systemic importance — BaseMiddleUpperTop. The ~15–16 largest, systemically important NBFCs are placed in the "Upper Layer" (NBFC-UL), which carries bank-like rules — and a hard mandate: list on the exchanges within 3 years of being classified.

Tata Capital was named in the NBFC-UL list in September 2022 → its listing deadline was September 2025. That is the sole reason it IPO'd. The debut:

  • Listed Oct 13, 2025 — a ₹15,512 Cr IPO (~$1.75bn) at a ~$15bn valuation: the largest-ever Tata Group IPO and the largest-ever NBFC IPO in India.
  • Structure: fresh issue (~21 Cr shares) + offer-for-sale (~26.6 Cr shares — partial exits by Tata Sons and IFC). Price band ₹310–326.
  • Side-benefit — it deleveraged: the fresh equity lifted the capital adequacy ratio toward ~20% (Tier-1 ~16%, up from ~12.8%), giving fresh headroom to grow.

The loan book

Retail-led, SME-heavy, thin corporate — plus a green-finance arm.
SegmentShare (FY25)What's inside
Retail~62%Home loans, loan-against-property, personal, business, vehicle, two-wheeler, education, microfinance
SME~26%Supply-chain finance, equipment financing & leasing, term loans, developer finance
Corporate~12%Larger structured / wholesale credit — deliberately kept small

Retail + SME = ~88% of gross loans (Mar-25). No single product exceeds ~20% of the book — the core diversification thesis.

Scale: gross loans grew from ₹1.58L Cr (FY24) → ₹2.22L Cr (FY25), +40.7%, reaching AUM ~₹2.77L Cr by Q4 FY26 (including Motor Finance).

  • Tata Cleantech Capital (green finance): the sustainable-finance arm funding renewables and clean-energy projects — a differentiated, ESG-aligned niche now run within Tata Capital.
  • Motor Finance: Tata Capital absorbed Tata Motors Finance — which is why results are reported both "ex-Motor" and "incl-Motor" (AUM ₹2.52L Cr ex-Motor vs ₹2.77L Cr incl-Motor). Motor is the lower-return drag being turned around.

07

The tension — is it worth the multiple?

The whole investment case in one screen.

▲ The bull case

  • The Tata brand — trust + one of the lowest costs of funds (~7.1%) in the sector.
  • Hyper-growth — ~37% loan CAGR into FY24, ~28% AUM growth now; guiding 23–25% CAGR.
  • Improving quality — GNPA already rolling over (2.33% → 2.0%); NNPA <1%.
  • Operating leverage ahead — C/I 36% → guided 33–34%; RoA guided 2.5–2.7%, RoE to 17–18%.
  • Diversified & de-risked — no product >20%; freshly capitalised (CAR ~20%).

▼ The bear case

  • Returns lag badly — RoE ~14% and RoA ~1.9% are the lowest of the peer set (Bajaj 22%, Chola 20%, Shriram 19%).
  • Rich valuation — P/B ~4.46x (richer than the ~3.4x often quoted), near Bajaj's multiple but on far lower returns.
  • Leverage — borrowings/equity ~5.3x (was ~6.5x pre-IPO); growth is debt-fuelled.
  • Motor-finance drag on blended returns during integration.
  • Execution risk — the price already assumes the RoE ramp happens.
The verdict to present: You're paying a premium ~4.5x book for a franchise that currently earns sub-peer returns. The bet is entirely on execution closing the gap — RoA lifting from ~1.9% toward the guided 2.5–2.7%, and RoE from ~14% toward 17–18%, powered by operating leverage and the Motor-finance turnaround. If Tata delivers, today's multiple is justified by the growth + brand; if the RoE ramp stalls, the stock is expensive for what it earns. The Tata name buys time and cheap funding that most NBFCs never get — that optionality is exactly what the market is capitalising.
