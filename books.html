Reading Notes · Investing
What I Learned About Investing from Darwin
by Pulak Prasad · Nalanda Capital
The Three Core Mantras
01
Avoid big risks
A great investor is first a great rejector.
02
Buy high quality at a fair price
Not growth at any price.
03
Be very lazy
Own businesses forever, trade almost never.
What Makes a High-Quality Business
  • ROCE — high historical ROCE is the single most reliable quality filter
  • Debt — debt-free or near debt-free; leverage kills long-term compounding
  • Moat — a strong, durable competitive advantage — not a temporary edge
  • Structure — fragmented customer and supplier base; no single point of dependency
  • Industry — slow-changing industry; stability is a prerequisite for compounding
  • Focus — one core product or service, or closely related businesses
  • Management — capable, aligned, with a clean and consistent track record
  • Resilience — must survive external shocks — cycles, downturns, disruptions
Non-Negotiable Avoidances
  • Promoters with integrity issues — disqualify immediately, no exceptions
  • Turnaround stories — past failure is signal, not opportunity
  • Highly leveraged companies — debt amplifies downside disproportionately
  • Companies with M&A tendencies — destroys capital, signals strategic insecurity
  • Rapidly evolving industries — structural disruption undermines any moat
  • Govt-owned companies, conglomerates, listed subsidiaries — misaligned ownership
How to Evaluate a Business
  • Interpret the present by studying the past — history is the only honest data source
  • Assess financials, strategy and competitive position through the historical record — not projections
  • Demonstrated, consistent earning power is the primary criterion — not forecasted cash flows
  • Rely on honest signals from the business — ignore analyst hype and market narratives
  • DCF is impractical — historical ROCE is a far more reliable, honest guide to value
  • Macro and economic forecasting is irrelevant — business fundamentals are what matter
  • Convergent thinking — recognise recurring patterns of success and failure; invest in proven business templates
On Valuation & Timing
  • Only invest when the market offers an attractive valuation — price paid determines return
  • Capitalise on inevitable short-term fluctuations — crises are buying opportunities in great businesses
  • Future projections are of little interest — the business must have already proven itself
  • Ignore valuation hype; focus on business quality — a great business bought expensively doesn't compound well
  • When a high-quality business hits short-term trouble — buy more, don't sell
Core Principle

Find an exceptional business with a long and consistent track record, buy it at a fair price, and own it indefinitely.

Reading Notes · Investing
Charlie Munger: The Complete Investor
by Tren Griffin · the operating system behind Berkshire Hathaway
The Four Graham Fundamentals
01
A share is part-ownership of a business
Value the business, not the ticker. If you can't value the business, you can't value the stock.
02
Buy with a margin of safety
A significant discount to intrinsic value is the protection against error and bad luck.
03
Mr. Market is your servant, not your master
His mood swings are the opportunity — never a source of instruction.
04
Be rational, objective, dispassionate
Temperament beats IQ. This is the hardest fundamental — and the one that powers the other three.
Worldly Wisdom — the Latticework
  • Mental models — borrow the big ideas from psychology, maths, biology, engineering, economics; one discipline is never enough
  • The hammer problem — "to the man with only a hammer, everything looks like a nail" — single-model thinking distorts reality
  • Circle of competence — the size of the circle matters far less than knowing exactly where its edge is
  • Inversion — "invert, always invert" — avoiding stupidity is easier and more reliable than seeking brilliance
  • Incentives — "never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives"
  • Simplicity — take a simple idea and take it seriously; complexity is not a signal of rigour
Psychology of Misjudgment — Biases to Guard Against
  • Incentive-caused bias — never take advice from someone paid to sell you the conclusion
  • Social proof — the crowd is what creates mispricing; following it guarantees average results at best
  • Availability & recency — the vivid and recent gets overweighted; the base rate gets ignored
  • Deprival superreaction (loss aversion) — losses hurt ~2x more than gains feel good, driving panic selling
  • Commitment & consistency — defending a past decision instead of updating on new evidence
  • Overconfidence & overoptimism — the more confident the forecast, the more suspicious you should be
  • Envy — "the world is not driven by greed; it's driven by envy" — FOMO destroys more capital than fear
  • Lollapalooza effects — biases rarely act alone; when several compound, behaviour goes badly wrong
Moats — Where Durable Advantage Comes From
  • Supply-side economies of scale — unit costs fall as volume grows; scale itself deters entry
  • Demand-side network effects — the product gets more valuable as more people use it
  • Brand — a promise that lowers the customer's search cost and earns a price premium
  • Regulation — licences and rules that raise the cost of entry for challengers
  • Patents & intellectual property — legally enforced exclusivity, while it lasts
  • Moats erode — capitalism attacks every excess return; test the moat continuously, never assume it
  • Quality over cheapness — Munger moved Buffett from Graham cigar-butts to paying a fair price for great businesses
The Right Stuff — Temperament of the Investor
  • Patience — "sit on your ass investing" — the big money is in the waiting, not the buying and selling
  • Decisiveness — bets are rare, but when the odds are heavily in your favour, bet big
  • Courage — be aggressive exactly when others are fearful — that is when prices are wrong
  • Sensibly contrarian — you can't beat the crowd by being the crowd; but contrarian and right are different things
  • Honest self-assessment — recognise mistakes fast, rub your nose in them, extract the lesson
  • Independence — work from facts and reasoning, not consensus, forecasts or authority
Non-Negotiable Avoidances
  • Acting outside your circle of competence — "I don't understand it" is a complete and honourable answer
  • Macro forecasting — nobody reliably predicts rates, markets or economies; ignore those who claim to
  • Leverage — the three ways a smart man goes broke: "liquor, ladies and leverage"
  • Overtrading and high fees — activity and costs are the silent killers of compounding
  • Envy-driven decisions — someone will always be getting richer faster than you; it does not matter
  • Trusting projections — they are made by people with an interest in the outcome; study the record instead
Core Principle

"It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."
