🧭 The one-line thesis
Copper is the metal of electrification — second only to silver as a conductor, and very hard to substitute. As the world electrifies (grids, EVs, renewables and now AI data centres), demand is set to climb ~50% by 2040, while mined supply struggles to keep up. That looming structural deficit is the heart of the copper investment case: a commodity where demand is increasingly visible but new supply is slow, costly and concentrated.
⚖️ The tension in one chart's worth of numbers

📈 The four demand vectors
S&P Global sees copper demand stacking up along four vectors (the "pyramiding call on copper") — together lifting demand from 28 Mt to 42 Mt by 2040:
1Core economic ("Dr. Copper") — construction, appliances, machinery, cooling. One of the two biggest drivers.
2Energy transition & addition — renewables, EVs, grid expansion, batteries. The other biggest driver.
3AI & data centres — the newest vector (didn't exist 4 years ago). See the next tab.
4Defence — electrified weaponry & systems; demand projected to triple by 2040 (NATO's 5%-of-GDP pledge).

Why demand keeps climbing
  • Electricity demand grows ~50% by 2040 — US +2.5%/yr, China +3.2%/yr, India +4.2%/yr.
  • An EV uses 2.9× the copper of a petrol car (~25 kg) — 22M EVs sold in 2025 vs ~10,000 in 2010.
  • Over 90% of new power capacity added in 2025 was solar & wind (~2.2 t copper per MW of solar).
  • The US designated copper a "critical mineral" (Nov 2025); humanoid robots are a possible new vector.
  • Asia ≈ 60% of all incremental demand, led by China & India.

🤖 Why "Copper in the Age of AI"
AI runs on electricity, and electricity runs on copper. Since ChatGPT (Nov 2022) the data-centre build-out has become a brand-new copper-demand vector. In 2025, roughly half of US GDP growth was attributed to AI spending, and US data centres could jump from ~5% to ~14% of US electricity demand by 2030. It isn't the biggest vector — but it's the fastest-emerging and it amplifies all the others (grid, renewables, batteries).

🔌 The grid behind the data centre
Beyond the building, connecting data centres to the grid needs copper too — S&P Global estimates ~1.0 Mt/yr of extra copper by 2040 just for data-centre power infrastructure (~0.5 Mt for the renewables hyperscalers buy via PPAs + ~0.5 Mt for transmission & distribution). The investable point: the AI build-out pulls on multiple parts of the copper chain at once — the data centre itself, the renewable power it buys, and the grid that connects it.

⛔ The supply gap — why it can't keep up
Without major new investment, primary mined supply peaks around 2030 (~27 Mt) then declines to ~22 Mt by 2040 as existing mines age — even as demand climbs to 42 Mt. That leaves a projected ~10 Mt shortfall. The causes: falling ore grades, rising costs, and an average 17 years from discovery to production (permitting, environmental reviews, local opposition). Recycling helps but can supply at best ~a quarter of demand by 2040 — it cannot close the gap. The takeaway: copper is structurally short for years, which keeps the price (and the value of refined metal) high.
⛏ Where copper comes from (mine supply)
  • Chile — ~23% of world mine output (the copper superpower)
  • DR Congo — ~14% (fastest-rising)
  • Peru — ~12%
  • China ~8% · USA ~5%
  • Just five countries hold over half of all reserves — supply is concentrated & slow to grow (declining ore grades, ~10-yr permitting).
🏢 The players
  • Miners: BHP (#1, >2 Mt FY25), Codelco (Chile, state), Freeport-McMoRan (US), Glencore, Antofagasta, Anglo American
  • Smelting: China refines ~50%+ of the world's copper — and is exactly who Kutch competes with for concentrate
  • India: Hindalco / Birla Copper (Dahej — ~50% of domestic supply), Vedanta / Sterlite (Tuticorin shut since 2018; Silvassa runs), Hindustan Copper (the only domestic miner). Kutch Copper is the big new entrant.

🔑 The concentrate squeeze — the heart of the story
A smelter doesn't buy finished copper — it buys copper concentrate (~25–30% copper) and earns a treatment & refining charge (TC/RC) for turning it into metal. That fee is a smelter's main income.
The problem: China massively over-built smelting capacity — it now holds 12 of the world's ~29 Mt of smelting capacity (~40–50% concentration, and still growing). Too many smelters chase too little concentrate, crushing TC/RCs to record lows: the 2026 annual benchmark settled at $0/tonne and spot TC/RCs have been negative since 2024 (~−$47/t in early 2026). Negative means a smelter effectively pays to process concentrate.
So smelters barely earn from refining copper today — they survive on by-product credits (gold, silver, sulphuric acid) and record-high refined-copper premiums. Chinese smelters even agreed to cut output ~10% in 2026. This China concentration is also a supply-chain risk the US & others now want to diversify — part of the strategic case for new smelters like Kutch.
🐂 The bull case
  • Structural deficit — demand heading to 42 Mt vs a ~10 Mt supply gap by 2040.
  • Electrification + AI — grids, EVs, renewables & data centres all copper-hungry.
  • Hard to substitute — only silver conducts better; easy "thrifting" is mostly done.
  • Critical-mineral status & years of under-investment in new mines.
🐻 The bear case
  • "Dr. Copper" — deeply cyclical; a recession or China slowdown hits demand fast.
  • Demand destruction — very high prices push some users to aluminium / fibre.
  • Recycling & new supply could surprise to the upside over time.
  • Policy, tariff & trade shocks given how concentrated the chain is.
💼 How investors get exposure
  • The metal — LME / COMEX futures, physical, or copper ETFs/ETCs (direct price exposure).
  • Miners — diversified majors (BHP, Glencore, Rio Tinto) or pure-plays (Freeport, Antofagasta, Ivanhoe); more leverage to the price.
  • Royalty & streaming — take a cut of output without operating risk.
  • Smelters & refiners — the squeezed middle (TC/RCs near zero) — risky unless integrated or by-product-rich.
  • Downstream & "picks-and-shovels" — cable/wire makers, recyclers, mining-equipment suppliers.
👀 What to watch
Mine-supply growth & new-project approvals · TC/RC levels (smelter health) · ore-grade trends · China demand & smelter cuts · exchange inventories · and the copper price vs the "incentive price" needed to justify new mines. When demand visibility is high but supply is slow, the metal and the best-positioned miners tend to do the heavy lifting — while the un-integrated smelter is the most exposed link.

🛠 What it is
Kutch Copper Ltd takes imported copper concentrate and turns it into 99.99% cathodes and rods/wire, plus by-products (gold, silver, sulphuric & phosphoric acid). When fully built (1 Mtpa by FY29) it would be the world's largest single-location copper smelter and roughly double India's refined-copper output. Open the parent — Adani Enterprises →
⚠️ First-year setbacks
A smelter this size needs ~1.6 Mtpa of concentrate — but reporting (Apr 2026) says Kutch imported only ~a quarter of that (Feb 2024–Feb 2026), and Earth-i satellite data saw little sustained smelting after mid-2025; high feedstock impurities (antimony, arsenic, uranium) were also flagged. Adani denies engineering problems, calling ramp-up "swift" and "steady." Contested reporting, not settled fact.
🏰 The bet & the moat
A play on import substitution (India imports much of its copper) + structural demand. The would-be moat: scale (lowest cost/t), Mundra's captive port & power, and by-product credits. The risk is the one in this study — securing concentrate at workable charges.
⚖️ Why this study matters for Kutch
Kutch has no captive mine, so it imports 100% of its concentrate straight into the squeeze covered earlier — a double bind:
  • Availability: scarce concentrate, bid up by Chinese smelters (it secured only ~¼ of needs in year one).
  • Margins: with TC/RCs at zero-to-negative, the core processing fee earns almost nothing.
  • What saves it: by-product credits (gold/silver/acid), record copper premiums, India import-substitution, Mundra's port + power, and locking long-term offtake (or buying a mine).
Bottom line: for an importer-smelter, the bull case (copper demand) and bear case (no feedstock, no margin) are the same trend from two ends — securing concentrate is the whole game.
