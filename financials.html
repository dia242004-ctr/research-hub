Section D
Key Metrics Dashboard
The 12 metrics every bank analyst tracks — formula, benchmark and red flag for each.
NIM Profitability
Net Interest Income ÷ Avg. Earning Assets
Good≥ 3.5%
Red Flag< 2.5%
Higher NIM = wider spread between lending & deposit rates. CASA-rich banks structurally enjoy higher NIM.
CASA Ratio Funding
CASA Deposits ÷ Total Deposits × 100
Good≥ 40%
Red Flag< 25%
Current + Savings deposits are near-zero cost. High CASA = low funding cost = structural NIM advantage.
GNPA Asset Quality
Gross NPA ÷ Gross Advances × 100
Good≤ 2%
Red Flag> 5%
Total bad loans as % of loan book. Falling GNPA = cleaner balance sheet = lower future credit cost.
NNPA Asset Quality
(Gross NPA − Provisions) ÷ Net Advances × 100
Good≤ 0.5%
Red Flag> 2%
Net NPAs after provisions — the true unhedged risk on the balance sheet. Must be below 1% for quality banks.
PCR Provisioning
Cumulative Provisions ÷ Gross NPA × 100
Good≥ 70%
Red Flag< 50%
Provision Coverage Ratio — how much of bad loans are already provided for. Higher PCR = less future P&L risk.
CD Ratio Liquidity
Total Advances ÷ Total Deposits × 100
Good70–80%
Red Flag> 90%
Credit-Deposit ratio — how much of deposits are lent out. Too high = liquidity stress; too low = growth opportunity missed.
CAR Capital
Tier 1 + Tier 2 Capital ÷ Risk-Weighted Assets
Regulatory Min11.5% (RBI)
Red Flag< 12%
Capital Adequacy Ratio — buffer against losses. Well-capitalised banks (CAR >16%) can grow without dilution.
Slippage Ratio Asset Quality
Fresh NPA additions in period ÷ Opening Standard Advances
Good≤ 1.5%
Red Flag> 3%
Rate at which good loans turn bad. A rising slippage ratio is the earliest warning signal of deteriorating asset quality.
Credit Cost Provisioning
Total Provisions for period ÷ Avg. Net Advances
Good≤ 1.0%
Red Flag> 2.5%
Provisions as % of loan book. The single biggest swing factor in a bank's P&L. Falling credit cost drives earnings upgrades.
ROA Returns
PAT ÷ Average Total Assets × 100
Good≥ 1.5%
Red Flag< 0.8%
Return on Assets — the cleanest profitability metric, unaffected by leverage. Best-in-class private banks target 2%+.
ROE Returns
PAT ÷ Average Shareholders' Equity × 100
Good≥ 16%
Red Flag< 10%
Drives P/B valuation directly. ROE > Cost of Equity (~14%) is needed to justify P/B > 1x. ROE = ROA × Leverage.
P/B Ratio Valuation
Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share
Private Banks2.0x – 4.0x
PSU Banks0.8x – 1.5x
Primary valuation metric for banks. Justified P/B = ROE ÷ (COE − g). High ROE + low GNPA → premium P/B.

Key NBFC Metrics Dashboard
Eight metrics that define NBFC quality — with NBFC-specific benchmarks distinct from banks.
AUM GrowthScale
AUM (end) − AUM (start) ÷ AUM (start) × 100
Good≥ 20% YoY
Red Flag< 10% or decelerating sharply
AUM growth drives revenue since NBFCs earn a spread on their book. Decelerating AUM = decelerating earnings. Best NBFCs sustain 25–30% AUM CAGR over cycles.
NIM / SpreadProfitability
Yield on AUM − Cost of Borrowings
Good (Diversified)5–8% spread
Red FlagSpread < 4% — structurally challenged
Unlike banks, NBFCs have no CASA — funding cost floor is the wholesale market rate. Spread compression during rate hikes is their Achilles heel.
GNPAAsset Quality
Gross NPA ÷ Gross AUM × 100
Good≤ 3%
Red Flag> 6%
NBFC GNPA benchmarks are higher than banks given borrower profile. Context matters — MFI GNPA of 5% is very different from HFC GNPA of 5%.
Credit CostProvisioning
Provisions ÷ Average AUM × 100
Good≤ 1.5%
Red Flag> 3.5%
Higher than banks given unsecured exposure. The biggest swing factor in NBFC PAT. Watch write-off trends — some NBFCs mask stress through write-offs.
Debt-to-EquityLeverage
Total Debt ÷ Net Worth
Good≤ 4x
Red Flag> 7x
Excessive leverage amplifies both returns and losses. IL&FS had D/E of 12x+ before collapse. RBI mandates Tier-1 capital norms for Upper Layer NBFCs.
ROAReturns
PAT ÷ Average AUM × 100
Good≥ 3.0%
Red Flag< 1.5%
NBFCs should earn higher ROA than banks (3–5% vs 1–2%) to compensate for higher funding cost and credit risk. Bank-like ROA without CASA = flawed model.
ROEReturns
PAT ÷ Average Net Worth × 100
Good≥ 18%
Red Flag< 12%
ROE drives P/B valuation. Best-in-class NBFCs (Bajaj Finance) sustain 20–25% ROE through cycle. ROE below cost of equity = value destruction.
P/B RatioValuation
Market Cap ÷ Book Value of Equity
Premium (Bajaj Finance)4–8x
Average NBFCs1.5–3x
NBFCs with consistent ROE >20%, AUM growth >25% and falling NPA command premium P/B. The market pays for compounding franchise value.

Section B
Life Insurance Product Mix — Margin Impact
Product mix is the single biggest driver of VNB margin. Richer protection mix = higher margins = higher EV growth.
ProductTypical PremiumVNB MarginRisk to InsurerMix Shift Impact
Term / Protection PlansLow annual (₹8,000–₹20,000/year)50–70% — highest marginMortality risk — large payouts on death↑ VNB margin expands significantly
ULIPsMedium-high (₹50,000–₹2L/year)10–20% (market-linked)Low — market risk borne by customer↔ Volume-driven; margin dilutive vs term
Savings / EndowmentMedium (₹30,000–₹1L/year)8–15% — lower marginsInterest rate risk on guaranteed returns↓ Margin dilutive; creates ALM risk
AnnuitiesLarge single premium (₹5L–₹50L+)3–8% — lowest marginLongevity risk + interest rate risk↓ Very negative for VNB margin
Why mix matters: An insurer shifting from 30% to 40% protection mix can see VNB margins expand by 300–500 bps. HDFC Life (~27% VNB margin) and Max Life (~28%) have consistently higher protection mix — this is their structural moat.

P/B Deep Dive — The Core Bank Valuation Metric
Formula
P/B = Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share
Equivalently: P/B = Market Cap ÷ Total Shareholders' Equity
The Justified P/B Equation
P/B = ROE ÷ (Cost of Equity − g)

SOTP — Sum of the Parts Valuation
For financial conglomerates (ABCL, Tata Capital, Piramal), SOTP is the only correct method. Each subsidiary is valued on its own metric — then consolidated with a holdco discount.
1
Identify All Subsidiaries
List every business segment. Separate listed from unlisted. For listed subs, market cap gives you a real-time check on your valuation.
↓
2
Assign the Correct Metric per Segment
NBFC → P/B | AMC → % of AUM | Life Insurance → P/EV | Health Insurance → P/GWP or P/E | Each segment has its own industry-standard multiple.
↓
3
Value Each Segment Independently
Apply the multiple to the appropriate metric for each segment. Cross-check against comparable listed peers where possible.
↓
4
Sum the Parts → Apply HoldCo Discount
Total subsidiary value = SOTP. Then apply a 10–20% holdco discount (for management layer cost, dividend leakage, governance complexity, and liquidity discount of unlisted subs).

Section F
DCF Overview — When It Works (and When It Doesn't)
4 Steps of DCF
1
Project Free Cash Flows (5–10 years)
Estimate FCF = Operating Profit − Capex − Working Capital increase ± tax
2
Choose WACC (Discount Rate)
WACC = Cost of Equity × E/(D+E) + Cost of Debt × D/(D+E) × (1−tax). For BSFI: typically 13–16%
3
Calculate Terminal Value
TV = FCF(n+1) ÷ (WACC − g). Terminal growth rate g = 4–6% for strong financials
4
Sum = Intrinsic Value
Intrinsic Value = PV of FCFs + PV of Terminal Value. Compare vs current market cap.
DCF Applicability in BSFI
✓ Use DCF for:
AMCs (fee income is highly predictable cash flow) | Diversified financial holdcos | Insurance holding companies where subsidiary dividends are the cash flow
✗ Avoid DCF for:
Banks (impossible to define "free cash flow" — lending IS the business) | Life insurers (EV is a superior DCF-equivalent already baked in) | MFIs / early-stage NBFCs (FCF too volatile)
⚠️ DCF Sensitivity Warning:
A 1% change in terminal growth rate or WACC can move DCF value by 20–40%. Always do scenario analysis (bull/base/bear) and check if your intrinsic value makes sense vs relative valuation.

RBI's Rate Corridor — The Three Key Rates
The RBI manages liquidity and overnight interest rates through three policy tools. Together they form a "corridor" that anchors all other interest rates in the economy.
MSF Rate
6.75%
Marginal Standing Facility
Ceiling of corridor — emergency overnight borrowing from RBI
↓ 25 bps
Repo Rate ★ Policy Rate
6.50%
Rate at which banks borrow overnight from RBI
The primary monetary policy lever. MPC votes on this.
↓ 25 bps
SDF Rate
6.25%
Standing Deposit Facility
Floor of corridor — rate RBI pays banks for overnight deposits
How the corridor works: Banks with excess liquidity park it at the SDF rate (floor). Banks needing funds can borrow at the MSF rate (ceiling). In normal times, banks transact near the Repo rate. When system liquidity is tight, overnight rates drift toward MSF; when surplus, they drift toward SDF. The 50 bps spread between SDF and MSF is the "rate corridor" — a tool of liquidity management.
Section B
CRR & SLR — Reserve Requirements
Two mandatory reserve ratios that banks must maintain — both are tools of monetary policy and financial stability, not just prudential norms.
CRR — Cash Reserve Ratio
Current: 4.00%
What it is: The % of Net Demand and Time Liabilities (NDTL — essentially total deposits) that banks must hold as cash with the RBI. This cash earns NO interest.

Why it exists: Ensures banks always have liquid cash to meet sudden depositor withdrawals. Acts as a first-line liquidity buffer.

Monetary policy tool: RBI raises CRR to suck out money from the banking system (tightening). Lowers CRR to inject liquidity (easing). A 50 bps cut in CRR releases ~₹1 lakh Cr into the system.
↑ CRR Less money to lend → credit growth slows, interest rates rise
↓ CRR More money to lend → credit growth accelerates, NIMs can improve
SLR — Statutory Liquidity Ratio
Current: 18.00%
What it is: The % of NDTL that banks must hold in liquid assets — government securities (G-Secs), gold, or approved securities. Unlike CRR, SLR earns interest (G-Secs yield).

Why it exists: Ensures banks always have assets they can liquidate quickly. Creates a captive buyer base for government bonds — finances government borrowing.

Effect on lending: High SLR = more assets locked in G-Secs = less available for private sector loans. RBI has gradually reduced SLR from 25% (2012) to 18% to free up credit for the economy.
↑ SLR Banks forced to buy more G-Secs → crowd out private credit
↓ SLR Banks free capital for lending → credit supply increases
Section C
Monetary Policy Transmission — How Repo Rate Moves Reach Your EMI
A Repo rate change doesn't immediately affect borrowing costs. It travels through a chain of markets and decisions. Understanding this chain is critical for predicting NIM impact on banks.
🏛️
RBI MPC — Policy Decision
Monetary Policy Committee (6 members: 3 RBI + 3 external) votes on the Repo Rate. Decision announced every 2 months. Also sets stance: Accommodative / Neutral / Withdrawal / Restrictive.
↓ Immediate
💵
Overnight Money Market (Call Money)
Call money rates and overnight MIBOR move instantly to align with the new Repo Rate. Banks adjust their inter-bank lending rates within hours of the MPC decision.
↓ 1–4 weeks
📋
MCLR / EBLR Adjustment (Bank Lending Rate Base)
Banks revise their MCLR (Marginal Cost of funds-based Lending Rate) monthly. External Benchmark-Linked Rates (EBLR — pegged to Repo or T-bill rate) reset quarterly. EBLR is faster to transmit than MCLR.
↓ 1–3 months
🏦
Deposit Rates Revision
Banks reprice their FD rates — this is slower and more discretionary. Banks under liquidity pressure raise deposits rates fast; banks flush with CASA deposits are slow to revise. This is where NIM compression or expansion occurs.
↓ 3–6 months
🏠
Home Loan / Auto Loan / Business EMI Repricing
Floating rate retail loans (home loans linked to EBLR) reprice automatically. Fixed rate loans don't change. For borrowers, a 50 bps Repo cut → ~₹30–₹40/month reduction per ₹10 lakh of home loan outstanding.
↓ 6–18 months
📊
Macroeconomic Impact (GDP / Inflation)
Lower rates → cheaper credit → more investment + consumption → GDP growth. But the lag is long. The RBI typically sees full transmission impact 12–18 months after a rate change — hence why monetary policy is "forward-looking."
